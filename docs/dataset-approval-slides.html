What the Best Submissions Did

Three Patterns from the Top

  1. They named a specific person. Not "policymakers." The RSA Director of Road Safety Strategy. When you name a person, every design decision has a filter. Team 09: RSA Director • Team 10: WFP Regional Director
  2. They showed the data, not just the topic. "$229 to $458.7 per tonne in 60 days." "r = -0.76." This proves you opened the files and found something. Team 10: FAO wheat prices • Team 06: Pearson correlation
  3. They found the counter-intuitive insight. "Ireland's road deaths are rising while Europe's are falling." The front page prints what surprises, not what confirms. Team 09: Ireland +28% vs EU -12.3% • Team 06: 7 unprotected EDs

Real-World Principle

The Front Page Test

Would a newspaper editor put your headline above the fold? If not, your insight is not sharp enough.

The New York Times, 2020

"An Incalculable Loss"

1,000 obituaries filling the entire front page. No chart. No dashboard. One number, rendered as human stories.

Lesson: The most powerful visualisation can be the simplest.
The Guardian, 2023

"The last satisfactory A&E in England"

One choropleth. Every hospital zone red except one. The absence of green IS the story.

Lesson: Sometimes the most powerful pattern is what is NOT there.
Try this now

Write your headline in 12 words or fewer. If you cannot, your story is not focused enough yet.

9DATAV CHALLENGE 2026
What to Fix

Three Gaps That Cost Marks

  1. No evidence of data exploration. Naming datasets is not enough. Show a statistic. Show a chart. Show you opened the file. The difference between "we plan to" and "we found X" is the difference between B- and A.
  2. No decision-maker. This is a Decision-Maker Influence Challenge. "Policymakers" is not a person. Name the role. Name the office. Name who would change their behaviour because of your work.
  3. Effort visible in word count. Top submissions: 1,200-1,500 words with figures. Weakest: 253 words. For a team of four working over a week, that gap is unmistakable.

Real-World Principle

Design for One Person, Not Everyone

The best data visualisations in the world were designed for a specific decision.

Florence Nightingale, 1858

The Rose Diagram

Designed for one audience: Queen Victoria and Parliament. One message: more soldiers die from infection than combat. One action: fund sanitation in military hospitals.

Result: Complete reform of military healthcare.
John Snow, 1854

The Cholera Map

Designed for one audience: the Board of Guardians of St James's Parish. One message: deaths cluster around the Broad Street pump. One action: remove the pump handle.

Result: The pump was removed. The epidemic stopped.
Your test

Can you complete this sentence? "After seeing my visualisation, [specific person] will [specific action]."

Craft Tip

Every Great Visualisation Has a Narrative Arc

Not just charts. A story structure your decision-maker follows.

1
Hook
The surprising number that makes them stop
2
Context
The trend or comparison that gives it meaning
3
Drill-Down
Where, who, or what is driving the pattern
4
So What
The action the decision-maker should take
Your Team 09 (Applied)

Hook: Ireland +28% road fatalities while EU -12.3%. Context: Missed 2024 target by 45%. Drill-down: July, October, December deadliest; rural roads 72%. So What: Three policy interventions for RSA Director.

Design Principles

Do This / Not That

Do

  • One chart, one message. If you cannot say what a chart proves in one sentence, simplify it.
  • Annotate the insight. Put a label on the thing that matters. "This is where it changed."
  • Use colour with purpose. Grey for context. Colour for the thing you want them to see.
  • Benchmark everything. A number alone is meaningless. Compare to a target, a peer, a year.

Don't

  • Pack 8 charts on one page. Density is not depth. It is clutter.
  • Use 3D, pie charts, or rainbow palettes. These obscure data, they do not reveal it.
  • Let the tool choose the defaults. Tableau defaults are for exploration, not for communication.
  • Show everything you found. Cut 80%. Show the 20% that tells the story.
9DATAV CHALLENGE 2026
Real-World Principle

Annotation Is the Difference Between a Chart and a Story

Amanda Cox (NYT data editor):

"The annotation layer is the most important thing we do. It is where the journalism lives."

A line chart with no labels says "here is data." The same chart with one callout arrow saying "This is where the policy changed" says "here is the story."

Practical Example

A chart of waiting list growth

Without annotation: "Outpatient waiting list Jan 2025 to Feb 2026."

With annotation: An arrow at month 8 labelled "EUR 420M released here. No impact." That arrow IS the insight. Without it, the chart is just a line going up.

Rule of thumb

Every chart should have at least one text annotation that a reader could screenshot and share.

Design Tip

The Grey + One Colour Rule

The most effective data visualisations use grey for everything that is context, and one colour for the thing they want you to see.

Financial Times Style

27 countries in grey. Ireland in red.

When every line is a different colour, nothing stands out. When 26 lines are grey and one is red, the eye goes straight to Ireland. The design IS the argument.

Your decision-maker has 5 seconds. Guide their eye.
Applied to Your Projects

Examples from this cohort

Team 09: EU countries in grey, Ireland in red = "we are the outlier."

Team 06: All EDs in grey, 7 unprotected ones in red = "these are the gaps."

Team 10: All commodities in grey, wheat in orange = "this is the shock."

Final Submission Test

The 5-Second Test

Show your front page to someone who knows nothing about your topic. After 5 seconds, ask them:

?
"What is this about?"

If they cannot answer, your title or headline is missing.

!
"What surprised you?"

If nothing surprised them, your insight is not visible enough.

→
"What should happen?"

If they have no idea, your call-to-action is missing.

If your front page fails any of these three questions, redesign it before you build anything else.

Pitfalls to Avoid

Five Things That Sink Final Submissions

  • The data dump. Showing everything you found instead of the 3-4 things that matter. Cut ruthlessly.
  • The missing "so what." Charts that describe a pattern but never say why it matters or what to do about it.
  • The screenshot dashboard. Embedding raw Tableau exports without any narrative, labels, or design thought.
  • The orphan chart. A visualisation that is technically correct but has no title, no source, no units, no benchmark.
  • The credibility gap. Citing statistics without naming where they came from. If I cannot verify your number, I cannot trust it.

Final Submission Checklist

Before You Submit: The Non-Negotiables

Content

  • Headline visible in first 5 seconds
  • Decision-maker named
  • At least one counter-intuitive finding
  • Every number has a source cited
  • Clear "so what" / call to action

Design

  • One chart = one message
  • Annotations on key data points
  • Colour used with purpose (grey + accent)
  • Titles are assertions, not descriptions
  • Passed the 5-second test
Pro tip: Assertion titles

Descriptive title: "Road fatalities in Ireland, 2019-2024"
Assertion title: "Ireland's road deaths rose 28% while Europe's fell"
The title should tell me what the chart proves, not what the chart contains.
