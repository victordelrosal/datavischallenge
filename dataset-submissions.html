Team 01 adopted the recommended idea directly, focusing on how geopolitical tensions around Iran produce immediate and measurable spikes in global oil prices, with the Strait of Hormuz as the central chokepoint. Datasets

Team 02 adopted the recommended idea with a robust multi-indicator composite stress index approach. Dublin was recently ranked the second most stressed city in the world following New York. The team tests this claim through five quantifiable proxy indicators drawn entirely from the Central Statistics Office (CSO) of Ireland, providing a transparent, reproducible methodology.

Datasets

  • CSO Well-being Survey (SILC): life satisfaction, depression, loneliness, financial stress by region
  • Census Commuting Data: average journey times, long commutes by county
  • Census Housing and Rent Data: average weekly rent by county
  • RTB Rent Index: standardised monthly rents for new/existing tenancies
  • Census Population Statistics: population density and demographics

Analytical Approach

The submission identifies six preliminary insights with specific statistics. Dublin rent ranges from EUR 2,143-2,483/month, which is 65-90% higher than Cork City (EUR 1,297) or Galway (EUR 1,292). Nationally, 38.6% of renters report low financial satisfaction versus lower rates for homeowners, and life satisfaction fell from 7.7 (2023) to 7.6 (2024). The team includes a data quality assessment section covering completeness, missing values, and consistency checks. They plan to merge all five datasets, calculate composite stress indices, perform correlation analysis between objective stressors and subjective wellbeing, and create interactive Tableau dashboards.

Decision-Maker & Key Message

City planners, health officials, policymakers, and housing regulators. The key message: Dublin's stress is not anecdotal; it is quantifiable across multiple independent indicators, and the pressure is radiating outward into surrounding counties.

Team 06 is the most analytically rigorous submission in the cohort, despite being the only 2-person team. They test whether socioeconomic deprivation and flood risk are systematically co-located in Ireland, reframing flood risk as a social justice narrative rather than a hydrological one. The merged dataset of OPW flood zone classifications and Pobal deprivation index covers 209 Electoral Divisions across 33 counties and 8 NUTS3 regions, with 18 variables.

Datasets

  • OPW Flood Zone Classifications (data.gov.ie): Zone A (>1% AEP), Zone B (0.1-1% AEP)
  • Pobal HP Deprivation Index: 209 Electoral Divisions, unemployment, education, lone-parent rates
  • Composite vulnerability score combining both sources

Analytical Approach

The team found no missing values across 209 records. The HP Deprivation Score ranges from -28 (most deprived) to +28 (most affluent). Of the 209 EDs, 114 (55%) sit in Zone A, 50 (24%) in Zone B, and 45 (21%) outside any flood zone. The headline finding: Pearson correlation r = -0.76 (strong negative), meaning more deprivation systematically aligns with higher flood vulnerability. Mean deprivation in Zone A is -11.4 versus -2.8 in Zone B and -4.1 in non-flood zones. The team identified the top 10 most vulnerable EDs (vulnerability score >9.0), concentrated in Dublin inner city and Cork City Northeast with average deprivation scores below -20. A critical policy gap: 7 of 31 Critically Vulnerable EDs (23%) lack any flood relief scheme, representing communities that are simultaneously the most at-risk and the least protected. Regional vulnerability scores: South-East 6.84, Midlands 6.68, Mid-West 6.66 versus Dublin Region 4.72, despite Dublin receiving disproportionate media and investment attention. Five figures support the analysis: deprivation by flood zone, scatter plot, regional vulnerability, box plots, and flood type breakdown.

Decision-Maker & Key Message

Government, local authorities, and community investment institutions. The message: flood risk is not equally distributed; it falls hardest on communities already struggling, and seven critically vulnerable areas have no protection at all.

Team 10 exemplifies "evolution done right". The original brainstorm proposed generic global food prices with vague framing and no specific decision-maker. The submission narrows to one commodity (wheat), one shock (Russian invasion of Ukraine, 24 Feb 2022), and one consequence (food security in import-dependent MENA nations). Ukraine and Russia supplied 30% of global wheat exports in 2021. The transformation from a generic data summary to a specific, time-bounded supply chain disruption analysis is exactly the kind of scope sharpening the feedback recommended.

Datasets

  • FAO Food Price Monitoring & Analysis (FPMA): monthly wheat prices, Jan 2018-June 2024 (78 observations)
  • UN FAO Cereal Import Dependency Ratio (CIDR): 25 major nations (Item Code 21035)
  • FAO Trade Statistics: food CPI inflation for 17 countries (2021-2023)

Analytical Approach

Pre-invasion average wheat price: USD 229/metric tonne. Within 60 days of invasion, prices surged to USD 458.7/tonne (a 58% gain). The six-month post-invasion average was USD 413.8/tonne versus pre-invasion USD 296.4/tonne, a USD 117.4 differential (39.6% increase). The shock persisted for 20 months, well beyond procurement system response capacity. Bubble chart analysis identifies six countries with cereal import dependency above 50% and high Ukrainian wheat share: Lebanon (81.4% import dependency, 49.8% Ukrainian wheat share), Libya (88.7%, 43.2%), Yemen (78.2%, 38.5%), Tunisia (67.4%, 54.6%), Egypt (62.3%, 32.1%), and Morocco (53.1%, 37.8%). These six nations collectively represent over 190 million people.
